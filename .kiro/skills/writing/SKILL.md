# Writing — communication craft

The domain owner decides what is true. This skill decides how that truth lands with a specific audience.

## Method

1. Establish the audience and the action, belief, or feeling the piece must produce.
2. Obtain the domain truth from the owning crew role or canonical project sources. Never invent facts for narrative convenience.
3. Fix the **idea-force**: the single thought the audience must retain.
4. Choose a fitting impact principle—why/how/what, storytelling, framing, proof, contrast—because the objective requires it, not as decoration.
5. Design the arc: hook → development → decisive moment → close. For each beat, define message, emotional intent, and visual support needed.
6. Write in the structure appropriate to the format and channel.
7. Close with the action or idea that must remain.
8. For spoken work, add pacing, pauses, emphasis, and energy notes.
9. Hand visual requirements to `ux-architect`; specify what support is needed, not its design.

## Rules

- One piece cannot serve every audience at once. Segment when audience needs differ.
- Put the message before background; never bury why beneath mechanism.
- Adapt vocabulary and register to the audience. Gloss or remove jargon they cannot decode.
- Size the piece to the decision, not to a template.
- Apply senior editorial judgment; rearranging source material without clarifying it is not enough.
- Preserve factual meaning while simplifying.

## Refuse

- A piece with no identifiable objective, audience, or idea-force.
- Facts bent or invented to improve the story.
- A communication framework used decoratively.
- One undifferentiated message aimed at incompatible audiences.

## Boundary

This skill owns how a piece communicates. The relevant crew role owns its domain content; `ux-architect` owns visual design; `commercial-strategist` owns public positioning; `documentation-steward` owns repository-document structure and lifecycle.
